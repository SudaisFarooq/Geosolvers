CASE STUDY – FLOODS IN UDHAM SINGH NAGAR DISTRICT, UTTARAKHAND
Detailed assessment of flood patterns, causes, and impacts in the Terai plains region

1. Introduction

Udham Singh Nagar (U.S. Nagar) is a low-lying Terai-plain district located at the Himalayan foothills of Uttarakhand, covering 3055 km² and bounded by Nainital district and Nepal to the north, Pilibhit & Bareilly to the east, Bilaspur and Moradabad to the south, and Bijnor to the west. The district lies between 28°–29° N latitude and 78°–81° E longitude.

The district is drained by rivers descending from the Shivalik ranges—Gaula, Kosi, Dhela, Fika, Devha, Kailash, Sukhi, Begul, and Nihai—that deposit enormous sediment loads in the plains. Because of this topography, the region experiences recurrent floods and waterlogging almost every monsoon season, particularly in Jaspur, Kashipur, Rudrapur, Sitarganj, Khatima, and Kichha tehsils.

2. Chronology of Major Flood Events

  • 1993 & 2000 (Gaula River): Severe flooding damaged the railway line near Kichha and inundated nearby areas.
  • 2010–2011 (Erosion at NH-34 & Sutaiyya Dak Bungalow): Intense erosion washed away 4–5 residential houses despite temporary embankment works.
  • 2013: The statewide Uttarakhand disaster (16–17 June) caused downstream flooding and debris accumulation in the Terai plains of U.S. Nagar.
  • 2021 (Gaula–Kichha Barrage Failure): Discharge exceeded 15 000 cusecs, breaching several barrage gates, inundating Kichha and surrounding villages.
  • 2022: Exceptionally heavy monsoon rains caused prolonged urban waterlogging across Rudrapur, Kashipur, and Sitarganj.

5.Causes of Flooding

a)Physical Causes of Flooding

Hydrometeorological Drivers: Studies show a 50% rise in cloudburst frequency since 2010 and 60–70% increase in monsoon intensity. These short-duration, high-intensity rains produce flash runoff beyond the carrying capacity of local rivers.

Topography and Drainage: The Terai plains have low gradients (~0.5–1 m/km). Rivers lose velocity as they enter the plains, depositing sediments and raising riverbeds. The shallow groundwater table leads to widespread inundation.

Siltation and Sediment Load: Rivers like Kailash, Devha, and Dhela carry large sediment loads, which raise riverbeds and reduce channel capacity.

Structural Failures: Barrage and embankment breaches (like the Kichha Barrage failure in 2021) exacerbate flooding when design limits are exceeded.

b)Anthropogenic and Land Use Drivers

Deforestation: Large-scale removal of forest cover in upstream catchments increases surface runoff and sedimentation downstream.

Rapid Urbanization: Rudrapur, Kashipur, and Sitarganj have expanded into floodplains, replacing absorbent soils with impervious surfaces, causing urban flooding.

Tourism Pressure: Construction along riverbanks for tourism reduces floodplain storage and disrupts drainage.

Drainage Congestion: Inadequate and clogged stormwater drains and sewage systems worsen flooding and waterlogging.

c)Socio-Economic and Infrastructural Impacts

Human Impacts: Seasonal displacement of thousands; post-flood outbreaks of waterborne diseases like dengue and malaria.

Infrastructure Damage: Floods in 1993, 2000, and 2010 damaged roads, bridges, and the Kichha railway line. In 2000, Kosi River erosion near Sultanpur Patti destroyed two high-tension poles. Temporary embankments near NH-34 failed in 2011, washing away multiple houses.

Agricultural Losses: Recurrent crop damage (paddy, wheat, sugarcane); siltation reduces soil fertility and productivity across thousands of hectares.

Economic Burden: Combined annual damage to crops, infrastructure, and relief expenditures runs into crores of rupees each monsoon.

d)Institutional Preparedness & Response

Udham Singh Nagar district maintains 11 management teams (each with a 24-hour ambulance), 2 control rooms, and flood posts at every tehsil. Sample posts include:

  • Rajpur (Jaspur) – Govt Primary School (Fika River)
  • Dhakiya Gulabo (Kashipur) – Primary School (Dhela River)
  • Police Station Sultanpur (Bajpur) – Kosi River
  • Sirauli Khurd (Kichha) – Gaula River
  • GIC Shaktifarm (Sitarganj) – Begul River

Despite these arrangements,it has been observed that permanent protective structures and long-term mitigation are still lacking in several sensitive areas.

6. Conclusion

The combined evidence from the analysed reasearch paper and district data shows that flooding in Udham Singh Nagar is a compound hazard driven by:

  • Intensifying monsoon rainfall and cloudbursts,
  • Low-lying geomorphology of the Terai plains,
  • Deforestation, unplanned urbanization, and poor drainage,
  • Structural inadequacies like outdated barrages and embankments.

Over 1.3 lakh people and 19,000 houses remain exposed every year. Disasters like the 2021 Kichha Barrage failure and 2022 Rudrapur flooding underline the urgency for resilient infrastructure and systematic floodplain management.
